# Voice — Centennial Family Dentistry

**Revision:** 1 · **Last updated:** 2026-07-22

How the practice sounds in writing. Every word of copy on the pitch pages
(Tasks 10–12) is checked against this file. This is voice *extraction*: the
practice already writes a certain way, and these rules name the pattern in
their own words so the pitch site reads like a better-edited version of them,
not a generic dental template.

Every claim is tagged `[OBSERVED]` (directly evidenced in the practice's own
copy or reviews) or `[INFERRED]` (an analytical read). Every `[INFERRED]` claim
is also logged in `voice_decisions.md` as `[PROPOSED]` — only the practice can
ratify an inference.

---

## Emotional engine

The private feeling the copy is written for, and the conviction underneath the
practice's own words.

- **Customer inner life** `[INFERRED]` — The reader is often someone who dreads
  the dentist (avoids it, puts it off, braces for the lecture) *and* dreads the
  bill they won't see until it's too late to say no. Frequently they are the
  one booking for a family, and the patient base skews toward older adults and
  anxious first-timers. What they want is to be treated as a whole person by
  people who know their name, and to know what something costs before they are
  in the chair. Evidence for the shape of this reader: the practice's own
  anxiety copy (Dr. Greene: *"I empathize with those who have dental anxiety"*;
  Kadi: *"working with people with anxiety"*) and its patients' words (Kailey:
  *"a chronic dentist-avoider due to dental anxiety"*; Tiffany: *"the dentist is
  most people's least fave place"*; Meagan: *"anyone who feels anxious about
  going to the dentist"*). The synthesis into one reader is the inference.

- **Core conviction** — Two threads, one belief: *you deserve to know what your
  care is and what it costs, up front.* The affordability/accessibility half is
  stated outright — the financing page opens *"We believe quality dental care
  should be accessible and affordable for everyone"* `[OBSERVED]`, and the
  practice prints actual dollar figures on every service page while reserving
  "call our office" for *"all other treatment inquiries"* only `[OBSERVED]`. The
  reading that this transparency is a stance about *respecting the patient's
  right to decide*, not just a pricing choice, is the inference `[INFERRED]`.

## Personality — three words

Each word carries a specific in-practice meaning, not its dictionary meaning.

1. **Named** `[OBSERVED]` — Care here has a face and a first name. The About
   page gives *everyone* a bio — all three doctors and all twelve staff — and
   the practice refers to its own dentists as "Dr. Eve," "Dr. Greene,"
   "Dr. Gulrajani" (routine-care page: *"Dr. Eve, Dr. Greene or Dr. Gulrajani
   typically take X-rays"*). Patients answer in kind, naming the specific person
   who helped them (Ashley: *"Dr. Greene were exceptional"*; Yoni:
   *"Dr. Gulrajani is the bestest"*; F G: *"Her assistant Jessica"*). The voice
   never dissolves people into "our team of professionals."

2. **Plain-spoken** `[OBSERVED]` — It says the number, names the procedure, and
   explains what happens in words a patient uses. Prices are printed, not
   hidden; the routine-care copy walks through an actual visit
   (*"removing tartar buildup… polishing surfaces with a fluoride paste"*)
   rather than advertising it. No hype, no superlatives about itself.

3. **Steadying** `[OBSERVED]` — The copy's job is to lower a nervous patient's
   pulse: name the dread, then explain the thing that dissolves it. The practice
   meets anxiety head-on (Dr. Greene: *"more than willing to work with you to
   determine the best way to provide you with comfortable care"*) and its
   patients report exactly that effect (Tiffany: *"immediately helped to put my
   mind at ease"*).

## Rules (10)

Each rule cites the source it was extracted from, or is marked `[INFERRED]`.

1. **Doctors and staff are named people, never "our team."** `[OBSERVED]` —
   About page bios everyone by name; routine-care names all three doctors;
   reviews praise specific named people. Collapsing them into an anonymous
   collective erases the practice's clearest signal.

2. **State prices plainly; never write "call for pricing" where a price
   exists.** `[OBSERVED]` — Every service page prints dollar figures; the only
   call-for-pricing line on the whole site (`/services-and-pricing`) is scoped
   to *"all other treatment inquiries,"* not to any named, priced service.

3. **Name the fear, then lower it.** `[OBSERVED]` — Acknowledge dental anxiety
   directly and answer it with what actually happens at the visit, the way the
   practice already does (Dr. Greene and Kadi bios) and the way patients say it
   landed (Tiffany, Kailey, Meagan).

4. **Explain, don't pronounce.** `[OBSERVED]` — The practice teaches: the meta
   description names *"prevention, education"*; routine-care copy is
   *"teaching you the skills you need to prevent tooth decay"*; hygienist bios
   promise *"patient education"* to *"empower you to make informed decisions."*
   Copy earns trust by explaining the *why*, not by asserting authority.

5. **Speak as "we" — one voice for a three-dentist office.** `[OBSERVED]` —
   Most pages already use "we"/"our office." One page (`/fillings-and-crowns`)
   slips into first-person singular (*"I try viewing each patient… contact me
   today"*); that is the error the rewrite corrects, not a voice to preserve.

6. **Treat the whole person, not the tooth.** `[OBSERVED]` — The practice's own
   framing: *"viewing each patient as a whole rather than a single symptom"*
   (fillings page); *"good overall health as well"* (sleep-apnea page);
   *"links between a healthy mouth and a healthy body"* (Dr. Eve bio); echoed by
   Tiffany: *"my overall health, not just my teeth."*

7. **Sound local and familiar, not corporate.** `[OBSERVED]` — Snohomish and
   hometown are load-bearing: Dr. Greene *"born and raised in Snohomish…
   returned to her hometown"*; Nena: *"such a wonderful dental office in my own
   town… a valuable asset to our area."* The voice belongs to this town, not to
   a dental group.

8. **Earn trust with specifics, not adjectives.** `[OBSERVED]` — A price, a
   name, or a plain account of what happens at the visit does the work that
   "state-of-the-art excellence" cannot. The practice's own copy is measured;
   the effusive superlatives all live in the reviews, where they belong.

9. **Let patients' own words carry the praise.** `[OBSERVED]` — Reviews are
   effusive (*"the best dental experience I've ever had"*); practice copy stays
   calm and factual. Keep that division — the practice states, patients gush —
   rather than manufacturing warmth in the practice's own voice.

10. **Rewrite freely; never re-fact.** `[INFERRED]` (construction — see
    Decisions) — Phrasing, grammar, structure, first-person→"we", and the
    site's genuine typos *may and should* be fixed. A **fact, price, name,
    credential, or patient quote may never change** — those come from
    `products.md` verbatim, and patient quotes keep their original typos. The
    line: rewrite *how it reads*, never *what it asserts*.

## Anti-patterns

What the pitch copy must never do. Each is tagged; `[INFERRED]` guardrails are
also logged as `[PROPOSED]` in `voice_decisions.md`.

- **No anonymous "our team of experts."** `[OBSERVED]` — Direct inverse of
  Rule 1. The practice names everyone; erasing that is off-voice.

- **No "contact us for pricing" euphemism when a real number exists.**
  `[OBSERVED]` — Inverse of Rule 2. The practice's transparency is a
  differentiator; hiding a known price throws it away.

- **No corporate-dental hype.** `[INFERRED]` — "Your smile is our passion,"
  "state-of-the-art excellence," and self-applied superlatives are absent from
  every captured page; the practice's register is plain. Importing hype would
  be inventing a voice they don't use.

- **No fear-based copy.** `[INFERRED]` — The practice sells reassurance, not
  dread (it answers anxiety, it doesn't manufacture it). Scaring a patient into
  booking inverts the whole emotional engine.

- **No fabricated warmth.** `[INFERRED]` — Do not invent feelings, anecdotes,
  families, or sentiment not present in the evidence. Warmth is shown by
  accurately surfacing what patients actually said, never by writing new
  sentiment in the practice's mouth.

- **No clinical, cold register.** `[INFERRED]` — Matches the visual family's
  "no medical blue" (visual `I6`). The entire evidence base is warm; importing
  sterile hospital diction is the verbal equivalent of the cliché the visuals
  already refuse.

- **No invented facts to fill a gap.** `[OBSERVED]` — Enforced by
  `products.md`'s gotchas: no star ratings, no "13 reviews," no "years serving
  Snohomish," and never a single made-up number for a service whose price the
  source site contradicts itself on.

## Messaging tiers

Responsibilities, not headlines. (Actual headlines and taglines are drafted in
Tasks 10–12 against these rules — not here.)

- **Brand-level** (the through-line every page must carry, however phrased): a
  *named, local team* who are *transparent about cost* and whose first job is to
  *lower the anxiety* of going to the dentist. If a page's copy could run under
  any dentist's logo, it has dropped the brand level — rewrite it. `[OBSERVED]`
  synthesis of the personality words and Rules 1, 2, 3, 7.

- **Page-level** (each page carries one job, in the brand voice):
  - **Home** — who they are and why a nervous patient is safe here; the named
    team and the reassurance lead.
  - **Services & pricing** — the plain-cost promise made good: real numbers,
    plainly explained, in the practice's own measured register.
  - **Financing** — the affordability path, extending the transparency
    conviction from "here's the price" to "here's how you can manage it."

  Page jobs are set by the three-page pitch scope; the tiering principle
  (brand constant + one page job each) is `[INFERRED]`.
